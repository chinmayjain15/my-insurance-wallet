# Go-To-Market Strategy — MyInsuranceWallet
### Version 1.0 · March 2026

---

## 1. Positioning & Messaging

### The Core Idea
MyInsuranceWallet is not an insurance product. It is a peace-of-mind product. The GTM must never feel like an insurance ad.

Most insurance marketing triggers anxiety — "what if something happens to you?" We do the opposite. The message is warm, proactive, and grounded in care for family. It belongs in the same emotional category as writing a will, setting up nominees, or creating a family emergency contact list. Things people feel *good* about doing.

### The Tone
**Not this:** "Are your loved ones protected if you're gone?"
**This:** "Spend 20 minutes this Sunday. Your family will always know exactly where to look."

The trigger is not fear. It is the quiet satisfaction of being organised — the same feeling as cleaning your inbox, backing up your phone, or finally filing your documents. It is a productive, caring act. Something a responsible adult does for their family on a calm afternoon.

### The Positioning Statement
> MyInsuranceWallet is the simplest way to make sure your family can find your insurance policies when they need them most — without you having to be there.

### What We Say
- "Everything in one place. Shared with the people who matter."
- "Set it up once. Your family is sorted."
- "The 20-minute thing you've been meaning to do."
- "Not for you. For them."

### What We Don't Say
- Anything that references death, hospitalisation, or crisis directly
- "Insurance management" — too corporate, too dry
- Anything that sounds like we are selling insurance
- Anything that implies complexity or effort

## 2. Target Audience

### Primary Segment
**Urban working professionals in India, aged 28–45, with a spouse and/or children.**

They hold at least one insurance policy — likely health (corporate or individual), term, and possibly vehicle. They are the primary earner or co-earner in their household. They are digitally comfortable — use apps like GPay, Zerodha, Cred, or similar. They care about their family's financial security but find insurance administration tedious and easy to defer.

### Why This Segment First
- High policy density — they likely hold 3–5 policies across different insurers
- High motivation — they have dependents who would be affected
- Digital-first — low friction to adopt a mobile web app
- Social — they share useful tools with their peer group naturally
- Reachable — they exist in identifiable online and offline communities

### Secondary Segment (do not target in Phase 1, but will arrive naturally)
**The trusted contacts** — spouses, parents, adult children added by the primary segment. They arrive via SMS invitation, not acquisition. Their onboarding must be frictionless but they are not the initial GTM target.

## 3. Launch Phases

### Phase 0 — Seeding (Months 1–2)
**Goal:** 50–100 real users. Validate the core flow end-to-end with people who will give honest feedback.

- Draw entirely from the founders' personal and professional network
- Target people who the founders can call and get on a 10-minute feedback call
- Prioritise users who have a spouse or parent they would naturally add as a contact — this validates the sharing flow, not just the upload flow
- No public launch, no public communication
- Manual onboarding is fine — founders can walk users through it directly
- Success metric: at least 60% of Phase 0 users have uploaded at least one policy AND shared it with at least one contact

### Phase 1 — Organic Launch (Months 3–5)
**Goal:** 500–1,000 users. Establish a small but engaged base through word of mouth and content.

- Soft public launch — announce on LinkedIn and relevant communities
- Begin content publishing (see Section 7)
- Lean on the built-in sharing loop (see Section 5) — every user who shares a policy is pulling in a new user
- Collect and publish 5–10 short testimonials from Phase 0 users
- Success metric: 40%+ of new signups come from a referral or SMS invitation (not direct)

### Phase 2 — Channel Expansion (Months 6–12)
**Goal:** 5,000+ users. Introduce structured channels beyond organic.

- Activate partnership channel (see Section 6)
- Explore community partnerships — apartment associations (housing societies), alumni groups, corporate HR teams
- Consider a "Gift this to your parents" campaign — children setting up the app for their parents' policies
- Evaluate whether a referral mechanic (invite a friend, both get something) makes sense given no monetisation
- Success metric: at least one repeatable non-network acquisition channel identified and producing consistent signups

## 4. Acquisition Channels

### Channel 1 — The Founders' Network (Phase 0)
The first 100 users come from direct outreach. LinkedIn connections, WhatsApp groups, former colleagues, college friends who are now working professionals with families. This is not a scalable channel but it is the most important one — it produces real feedback, not vanity metrics.

### Channel 2 — The Product's Own Sharing Loop (Phase 1 onwards)
Every time a policyholder shares a policy with a contact, that contact receives an SMS and is pulled into the product. This is the most powerful acquisition channel and it costs nothing. It is also highly qualified — someone who receives a share invitation from a trusted person is far more likely to sign up than a cold user. Design and copy must make this moment as compelling as possible (see Section 5).

### Channel 3 — LinkedIn Content (Phase 1 onwards)
The primary content channel. Urban working professionals in India are active on LinkedIn. Short, useful, non-preachy posts about family financial preparedness — the kind that get shared because they make the sharer look thoughtful, not because they are promotional. The founders' personal profiles are the channel, not a brand page.

### Channel 4 — WhatsApp & Peer Sharing (Phase 1 onwards)
A well-positioned post or message that is easy to forward. "This took me 15 minutes and I feel so much better about it" is the kind of message that gets shared in family WhatsApp groups and peer circles naturally. The product must have a clean, shareable link that works well when forwarded.

### Channel 5 — Niche Online Communities (Phase 1 onwards)
Communities where the target segment discusses personal finance, family planning, or life organisation. Reddit (r/IndiaInvestments, r/personalfinanceindia), local Facebook groups for housing societies, Telegram groups for personal finance. Not promotional — participatory. Answer questions, add value, let the product speak for itself.

### Channel 6 — Partnerships (Phase 2 onwards)
See Section 6.

## 5. The Built-in Growth Loop

The sharing mechanic is not just a feature — it is the primary growth engine.

**How it works:**
1. A policyholder uploads their policies and shares with a contact
2. The contact receives an SMS: *"[Name] has shared their insurance policies with you on MyInsuranceWallet so you can access them when needed. Sign up here: [link]"*
3. The contact signs up — they are now a user
4. As a user, they see their own policies are not organised or shared with anyone
5. They become a policyholder themselves — uploading their own policies and sharing with their family
6. The loop repeats

This is a **pull loop**, not a push one. Nobody is being sold to. The contact signs up because someone they trust has done something thoughtful for them. That is a fundamentally different and more powerful acquisition dynamic than any ad campaign.

**What makes this loop work well:**
- The SMS copy must feel personal and warm, not automated
- The sign-up flow for incoming contacts must be extremely fast — they are motivated in the moment; any friction kills the conversion
- The empty state after a contact signs up must immediately surface the idea of uploading their own policies

## 6. Partnerships

Partnerships are a Phase 2 channel — not because they are unimportant, but because they require the product to be stable and the value proposition to be proven first.

### Independent Financial Advisors (IFAs) and Insurance Agents
**Why:** They already have a trusted relationship with policyholders. They help clients buy policies but often struggle with the "what happens to my family if I'm gone" conversation. MyInsuranceWallet gives them a tangible tool to offer clients — not a product to sell, but a service to recommend.

**How to approach:** Direct outreach to IFAs in the founders' network first. Offer them a simple way to recommend the product to clients — a referral link, a short explainer they can forward, or even a co-branded message they can send. No revenue share needed in V1 — the value to the IFA is being seen as thoughtful and client-centric.

### Corporate HR Teams
**Why:** Companies with group health insurance have employees who are exactly the target segment. HR teams are always looking for meaningful employee benefits that cost nothing. MyInsuranceWallet as a "recommended tool" in an HR newsletter or onboarding pack is a zero-cost, high-reach channel.

**How to approach:** Start with companies where the founders have connections. Pitch it as a financial wellness tool for employees, not as a product partnership.

### Housing Society Associations (RWAs)
**Why:** Apartment communities in Indian cities are tightly knit, have active WhatsApp groups, and often share useful services with residents. A recommendation from a trusted RWA secretary or committee member carries significant social proof.

**How to approach:** One founder personally reaching out to 5–10 housing society contacts with a simple, human message. Not a formal pitch — a neighbour recommending something useful.

### CA and Tax Filing Firms
**Why:** CAs interact with clients during tax season when insurance premiums and policies are already top of mind. A simple mention — "by the way, have you heard of this?" — from a trusted CA can convert.

## 7. Messaging & Content Direction

### Platforms
- **LinkedIn** — primary. Founders post as individuals, not as a brand.
- **WhatsApp** — secondary. Shareable messages and links, not a broadcast channel.
- **Instagram** — optional, low priority in V1. Visual format could work for the "Sunday productivity" angle but requires more effort.
- **Twitter/X** — low priority for this audience and message.

### Content Themes

**Theme 1 — The "I finally did this" post**
First-person posts from the founder's perspective: "I spent 20 minutes last Sunday uploading all my insurance policies and sharing them with my wife. Here's why I think every working professional should do the same." These perform well on LinkedIn and are easy to share.

**Theme 2 — The quiet problem**
Short posts that surface the insight without being morbid: "Your family has your policies. But do they know where they are? Do they know what to do with them?" Not fear — just a useful question that makes people pause.

**Theme 3 — The social proof story**
As users start using the product, short stories (with permission) about why they signed up, what they uploaded, and how it felt. Not testimonials — stories. "My father passed last year and we spent three weeks trying to find his life insurance policy. I just spent 15 minutes making sure my kids will never have to do that."

**Theme 4 — The "useful Sunday" prompt**
Seasonal or recurring content: "Three things worth doing this Sunday for your family's financial safety." MyInsuranceWallet is one of the three. This is shareable, non-promotional, and positions the product in a category of responsible, caring actions.

### Tone Guidelines
- First person, warm, unhurried
- Never preachy or guilt-inducing
- Never technical or insurance-jargon heavy
- Short — posts under 150 words perform better for this message
- No stock photos of sad families or hospital corridors

## 8. Success Metrics

### Phase 0 (Months 1–2)
| Metric | Target |
|---|---|
| Total signups | 50–100 |
| Users who uploaded at least 1 policy | 70%+ |
| Users who shared with at least 1 contact | 50%+ |
| Contacts who signed up after receiving a share | 30%+ |
| Qualitative feedback sessions completed | 10+ |

### Phase 1 (Months 3–5)
| Metric | Target |
|---|---|
| Total signups | 500–1,000 |
| % signups from referral / SMS invitation | 40%+ |
| Weekly active users (viewed or uploaded a policy) | 20%+ of total |
| Policies uploaded per active user | 2+ |
| Contacts added per active user | 1.5+ |

### Phase 2 (Months 6–12)
| Metric | Target |
|---|---|
| Total signups | 5,000+ |
| At least one repeatable non-network channel identified | Yes |
| Partnership introductions initiated | 5+ |
| Net Promoter Score (informal survey) | 50+ |

### The One Metric That Matters Most
**A user who has uploaded at least one policy AND shared it with at least one contact.**

This is the activated user. Everything else is vanity. A user who signed up but never uploaded is not a real user. A user who uploaded but never shared has not yet experienced the product's core value. GTM success is measured by the number of activated users, not total signups.
